5 Quoting Mistakes That Cost You Jobs

You're losing jobs and don't know why. It's probably not your price. Here are the mistakes contractors actually make—and how to fix them.

January 2026 6 min read

I talk to contractors every week who can't figure out why they're losing jobs. They assume it's price. So they drop their rates. Then they lose at the lower price too.

The problem usually isn't your number. It's everything around the number.

Here are the five quoting mistakes I see constantly—and the fix for each one.

1. Taking Too Long to Send the Quote

This is the biggest one. And it's the easiest to fix.

When a homeowner reaches out for a quote, they're excited. They've decided to do the project. They're picturing the finished deck, the new bathroom, the repainted kitchen. That excitement peaks right after your site visit.

78%
of jobs go to the first contractor who follows up with a quote

Every day you wait, that excitement fades. By day three, they're not thinking about their dream bathroom anymore. They're wondering why you haven't called. By day five, they've started looking at other contractors.

The client who was "definitely going with you" has moved on. Not because of price. Because you were slow.

The fix

Send your quote the same day. Yes, really. Even if it's 10pm. A quote at night is better than a quote in three days. The bar is so low here—most contractors take a week. Be the one who doesn't.

2. Sending a Quote Without Explaining It

Here's what a lot of quotes look like:

What clients receive

"Kitchen remodel: $15,000"

What clients need

Detailed breakdown showing labor, materials, timeline, what's included, what's not, and why things cost what they cost.

A single number with no context triggers suspicion. "Why does it cost that much? What exactly am I paying for? Is this guy trying to rip me off?"

You know why it costs what it costs. You've done the job a hundred times. But your customer hasn't. They need you to explain it.

Breakdown wins trust

When you itemize your quote—labor, materials, permits, disposal—you're not just justifying the price. You're showing you've thought it through. That's what separates pros from amateurs.

3. Looking Unprofessional

I know this one stings. But I have to say it.

If your quote is a text message, a napkin, or a handwritten note on the back of your business card, you're losing jobs to contractors who look more put-together.

Homeowners don't know how to judge your work. They can't look at your quote and evaluate whether you're a good tile setter. So they use proxies. They ask: "Does this person seem organized? Do they seem like a real business?"

A professional-looking quote signals:

  • You're established — not someone doing this on the side
  • You're organized — you'll show up, communicate, finish the job
  • You take yourself seriously — so should they

Is this fair? Maybe not. But it's how people make decisions.

The fix

Your quote should be a PDF. It should have your logo, your contact info, a clear breakdown of work, and a professional layout. It takes the same amount of time as texting a number—you just need the right tool.

4. Not Following Up

You send the quote. You hear nothing. So you assume they went with someone else and move on.

Wrong.

Here's what actually happened: They got your quote, meant to respond, then got busy. Their kid had soccer practice. Work was crazy. The quote is still sitting in their email, marked unread, because they're going to "deal with it later."

Later never comes unless you prompt it.

60%
of quoted jobs require at least one follow-up before the client responds

A simple "Hey, just checking if you had any questions about the quote I sent" is often all it takes. You're not being pushy. You're being professional. And you're reminding them that their project—the one they were excited about—is still waiting.

Don't overdo it

One follow-up after 2-3 days is helpful. A second follow-up after a week is fine. More than that starts to feel desperate. Know when to let it go.

5. Competing Only on Price

If you think you have to be the cheapest to win, you've already lost.

Price-focused clients are the worst clients. They'll nickel-and-dime every change order. They'll leave bad reviews over things that aren't your fault. They'll refer you to other cheap clients.

The clients worth having aren't looking for the lowest price. They're looking for the right contractor. Someone who:

  • Responds quickly and professionally
  • Explains the work clearly
  • Seems trustworthy and competent
  • Makes them feel confident about their decision

These clients will pay more for peace of mind. They've been burned before. They're not looking for a deal—they're looking for someone who won't make their life difficult.

When you compete on price, you attract price shoppers. When you compete on professionalism, you attract people who value quality.

The fix

Stop apologizing for your prices. Stop automatically offering discounts. Instead, focus on being fast, clear, and professional. The right clients will pay a premium for that—and they're much better to work with.

The Common Thread

Notice what all five mistakes have in common? None of them are about the actual number.

Contractors obsess over pricing. Should I charge $4,500 or $4,200? Is my hourly rate competitive? What if I'm too expensive?

Meanwhile, they're sending quotes three days late, with no breakdown, via text message, with zero follow-up. Then they wonder why the cheap guy won.

He didn't win because he was cheap. He won because he was fast, professional, and persistent. His quote looked better, arrived sooner, and he followed up when you didn't.

Fix those five things and you'll win more jobs at higher prices. I've seen it happen over and over.
