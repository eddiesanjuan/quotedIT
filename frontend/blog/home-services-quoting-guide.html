Home Services Quoting Guide: Close More Jobs

Whether you're cleaning homes, maintaining lawns, or fixing HVAC systems, this guide shows you how to price services, handle on-site estimates, and send quotes that win jobs.

December 24, 2025 12 min read Complete Guide

You just finished walking through a potential client's home. They're waiting for a number. You glance around, do some mental math, and throw out a price.

Sound familiar?

This approach — pricing on the spot without a system — is why most home service businesses struggle with profitability. You end up either too high (and lose the job) or too low (and lose money).

The solution isn't guessing better. It's having a system.

This guide covers quoting for every major home service: cleaning, landscaping, pest control, HVAC, handyman work, and more. You'll learn how to calculate costs, present prices, and close more jobs — profitably.

The Home Services Quoting Challenge

Home services quoting is uniquely difficult because:

  • Every job is different — A 1,200 sq ft home with 2 dogs is not the same as a 1,200 sq ft home with no pets
  • On-site pressure — Customers expect a number before you leave
  • Low barrier competition — Someone on Craigslist is always cheaper
  • Hidden costs — Travel time, equipment, callbacks — they add up
  • Price sensitivity — Residential customers shop around more than commercial

The businesses that thrive are the ones that have clear pricing structures, communicate value effectively, and present professional quotes.

Know Your Numbers First

Before pricing any job, you need to know your baseline costs:

1. Your Minimum Hourly Rate

Hourly Rate Calculation

Target annual income: $70,000

+ Self-employment tax (15%): $10,500

+ Insurance: $3,000

+ Vehicle costs: $8,000

+ Equipment/supplies: $4,000

+ Marketing: $2,500

= $98,000 needed

 

Billable hours: 1,600/year (80% utilization)

÷ $98,000 = $61/hour minimum

If you're charging $25/hour, you're losing money. Most solo home service pros need $50-75/hour minimum to be profitable.

2. Time Per Job Type

Track your actual time, including:

  • Travel — Door to door, not just on-site
  • Setup/teardown — Loading equipment, prep work
  • The actual work — What you bill for
  • Callbacks and follow-ups — Average per customer

A "1-hour cleaning" might actually be 2.5 hours of your day when you include travel and setup.

3. Materials Cost Per Job

Track what you use:

  • Cleaning supplies per clean
  • Fertilizer/chemicals per lawn
  • Consumables (filters, caulk, etc.)
  • Disposal fees

The On-Site Estimate Process

Most home service estimates happen at the customer's property. Here's how to run them professionally:

1. Before You Arrive

  • Confirm appointment (text or call 30 min before)
  • Look up property on Google Maps (size, access, condition)
  • Review any notes from the inquiry
  • Have pricing calculator/app ready

2. During the Walkthrough

  • Listen first — Ask what problems they want solved
  • Take notes — Room sizes, special conditions, concerns
  • Take photos — For your records and the quote
  • Identify upsells — "While I'm here, I noticed your gutters need attention..."
  • Set expectations — Timeline, noise, access needs

3. Presenting the Price

Don't just throw out a number. Frame it:

Professional On-Site Presentation

"Based on what we discussed — the bi-weekly full cleaning for your 2,200 sq ft home with 3 bathrooms and a focus on the kitchen — here's what I recommend:

 

Your first clean is a deep clean to get everything to baseline: $280

Then bi-weekly service at $185 per visit.

 

That includes all supplies, and if anything isn't right, we come back and fix it free. Most of my clients in this neighborhood pay between $175-200 for similar homes.

 

Want to get the first clean on the calendar?"

Key elements: summarize scope, anchor with context, make it easy to say yes.

4. After the Visit

  • Send written quote within 2 hours (same day is best)
  • Include photos if relevant
  • Follow up in 24-48 hours if no response
  • Track which quotes convert (and why)

What Your Quote Should Include

1. Clear Service Description

What exactly will you do? Be specific:

Too Vague

House cleaning - $175

Specific

Bi-Weekly Standard Cleaning

2,200 sq ft | 3 bed | 2.5 bath

 

- All floors vacuumed and mopped

- Dust all surfaces and ceiling fans

- Clean and disinfect bathrooms

- Kitchen counters, sink, appliance fronts

- Empty trash bins

- Make beds (linens left out)

2. Pricing Breakdown

  • Service fee
  • Materials/supplies (if separate)
  • Any add-ons or extras
  • Discounts applied
  • Total due

3. What's NOT Included

Prevent misunderstandings:

  • Interior of oven (available as add-on)
  • Exterior windows
  • Garage or outdoor areas
  • Laundry or dishes

4. Schedule & Access

  • Proposed date/time
  • Estimated duration
  • Access requirements (key, code, someone home)
  • Parking/access instructions

5. Payment Terms

  • Amount due
  • When payment is due (on completion, invoice, prepay)
  • Accepted payment methods
  • Late payment policy

6. Guarantee/Warranty

This builds trust:

Satisfaction Guarantee

"If you're not 100% satisfied with any area of our cleaning, let us know within 24 hours and we'll come back and re-clean it for free."

Handling Common Objections

"That's more than I expected"

"I understand — can I ask what you were expecting? There might be ways to adjust the scope to fit your budget while still getting the core service done well."

"The other guy quoted less"

"There's always someone cheaper. The question is: are they insured, do they guarantee their work, and will they still be in business next year? Here's what you get with us..." (Then reinforce your value props)

"I need to think about it"

"Of course. What questions can I answer to help you decide? I'll send over the written quote — it's valid for 7 days."

"Can you do it today/right now?"

"My schedule is pretty full today. I can fit you in at [next available time]. If it's urgent, I do offer same-day service at a 25% premium — would that work for you?"

Building Recurring Revenue

One-time jobs are hard work. Recurring clients are where the real money is:

  • Cleaning: Weekly or bi-weekly service
  • Lawn care: Weekly mowing contracts
  • HVAC: Annual maintenance plans
  • Pest control: Quarterly treatments

How to Pitch Recurring

  1. Do the first job exceptionally well
  2. At completion, say: "If you want to keep it this way, I can put you on a regular schedule."
  3. Offer a small discount for commitment (5-10%)
  4. Make it easy: "Same day each week, I'll have a key, you don't have to think about it."
The Math of Recurring

A bi-weekly cleaning client at $160/visit = $4,160/year. 10 recurring clients = $41,600 in predictable annual revenue. That's before you even take on new work.

Key Takeaways

  1. Know your numbers — Calculate your true hourly cost before pricing anything
  2. Use packages — Tiered options increase average ticket size
  3. Be specific — Detailed quotes prevent scope disputes
  4. Present professionally — Written quotes close more than verbal numbers
  5. Follow up fast — Same-day quotes convert 2-3x better than next-day
  6. Build recurring — Maintenance contracts are more valuable than one-time jobs

The home service businesses that thrive aren't necessarily the cheapest — they're the most professional. Start with your quote.
